12 — WHAT'S HAPPENING IN TECH

Micron's Historic Quarter — AI Memory Demand Is Not Slowing

Micron reported Q3 revenue of $41.5bn — a 340% year-on-year increase — with EPS of $25.11 vs $20.78 expected, and guided Q4 revenue to ~$50bn. The results confirm that AI data centre build-out is accelerating memory demand far beyond prior estimates. Qualcomm, separately, doubled its 2029 non-handset revenue target to $40bn, citing AI-on-device and automotive as the growth vectors.

Why it matters: The AI infrastructure build-out is running ahead of consensus. Every AI model improvement drives higher memory requirements — the cycle is in early innings. Bearish implication: DRAM price hikes are passing to consumer hardware (see AAPL/MSFT).

China Announces $295bn Five-Year AI Infrastructure Plan

The Chinese government unveiled a $295 billion, five-year AI infrastructure investment plan — approximately $59 billion annually in government-directed AI compute, data, and network build-out. The plan is intended to compete with US AI dominance and position China's tech ecosystem ahead of the 2030 global AI race.

Why it matters: Demand for chips, data centre components, and electricity infrastructure will receive a structural boost. Positive read-through for the broader semiconductor and data centre supply chain globally.

Qualcomm in Early Talks to Acquire AI Chip Startup Tenstorrent

Qualcomm is reportedly in early-stage acquisition discussions with Tenstorrent, the AI chip startup founded by chip design veteran Jim Keller. Tenstorrent's RISC-V based architecture is regarded as a promising alternative to Nvidia's CUDA-centric GPU stack. An acquisition would give Qualcomm a credible AI hardware position beyond its existing SoC business.

Why it matters: The AI chip market is consolidating rapidly. This deal, if completed, signals that hyperscalers and chip companies are serious about reducing Nvidia dependency — a potential long-term headwind for NVDA's moat.

White House Issues AI Innovation and Security Executive Order

The White House signed a new executive order in June 2026 focused on promoting advanced AI innovation while establishing security guardrails for frontier AI systems. The order targets national security applications, export controls, and a framework for evaluating AI safety at scale — balancing US innovation leadership against dual-use risk.

Why it matters: Regulatory clarity for AI companies in the US is emerging. Export restrictions could benefit domestic AI hardware companies and constrain Chinese access to advanced chips — a net positive for US-listed AI infrastructure plays.
